Functional Groups in Organic Chemistry

Functional groups are specific atoms or groups of atoms within molecules that determine the chemical properties of those molecules. These functional groups are like the "active sites" of organic compounds, governing how they behave in reactions.

Key Functional Groups

  • -OH (Hydroxyl group): Found in alcohols, such as ethanol. Hydroxyl groups make compounds more soluble in water.
  • -COOH (Carboxyl group): Found in carboxylic acids like acetic acid. It imparts acidic properties to the compound.
  • -COO- (Ester group): Found in esters, responsible for fruity smells like those found in many perfumes.
  • -NH2 (Amino group): Found in amino acids, which are the building blocks of proteins. This group imparts basic properties to the molecule.
  • -C=O (Carbonyl group): Found in aldehydes (e.g., formaldehyde) and ketones (e.g., acetone). It makes molecules more reactive due to its polar nature.

Why are Functional Groups Important?

Functional groups define the reactivity, polarity, phase of matter, and interactions of organic molecules. For example, alcohols (with hydroxyl groups) are often soluble in water, while ketones (carbonyl groups) are reactive with other chemicals to form different compounds.

Quick Quiz

Which functional group gives compounds their acidic properties?

Incorrect. Try again or click "Why?" for more information.

The correct answer is the Carboxyl group (-COOH) because it is present in carboxylic acids, which are acidic due to their ability to donate a hydrogen ion (H+).
